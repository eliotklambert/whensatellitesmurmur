Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Introduction


‘When Satellites Murmur’ is an ode to murmuration - the result of sonic experimentation between satellites, starlings, theremins, and (least importantly) humans. Murmuration as the sound made by thousands of starlings flapping their wings in unison, but more widely defined as the utterance of a low and continuous sound. In this paper, murmuration represents the ephemeral and at times incomprehensible channels of nonhuman vocalization. The work translates and modulates real-time and prerecorded signals between satellites and theremins, positioning their antennas not as passive receivers but as active listeners and instruments.


This is an invitation to decenter human perception and acknowledge that most nonhuman entities—whether biological or technological—communicate through sensory modalities we can never fully access, but can only attempt to translate or understand.


I am writing this paper in the US in 2025, where satellite technology has been developed by SpaceX to create a network of global surveillance for the US government, under the guise of achieving global connectivity for its customers. To make matters worse, the new Trump administration is simultaneously undermining the use of satellite technology for scientific research; for instance, imposing cuts on the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) which uses satellites to predict weather data.


And yet, despite these technopolitics, we are still captivated by the dawn chorus, by mysterious satellite signals, by aerial murmurs of both the natural and artificial kind. The ephemerality of our cosmic soundscape is a lesson in deep listening, a chance to liberate technologies like radio and satellites from their military origins, and to repurpose them to celebrate the wonders of the nonhuman world. What sonic communication can be imagined between satellites, birds, and humans - one of harmony or interference?


‘When Satellites Murmur’ explores this tension between the poetics of nonhuman listening and the politics of communication technology. The first section of the paper provides context for this exploration through the history of satellites, the relationship between satellites and their avian counterparts - starlings - and the world of deep listening and software defined radio. The second section documents my process of building a network between satellites, radio, theremins, and birds for my final installation.


This paper offers a momentary portal into the weird and wonderful world of the sky and its nonhuman networks.
