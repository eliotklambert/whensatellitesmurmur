Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Part 1 - Satellite Signals

I followed Open Weather’s toolkit to set up the satellite ground station on my roof in Brooklyn, in the attempt to pick up and record the signals of NOAA satellites.The toolkit teaches you how to set up a DIY satellite ground station to pick up signals and collect weather data through sound and image. The first step was to buy an SDR kit, which is an antenna connected to an SDR dongle that connects to your laptop and CubicSDR . CubicSDR is an open source interface that enables users to explore the radio spectrum and demodulate the frequencies that they pick up. All I needed to do was set the frequency and desired bandwidth of my intended signal, and I could pick up and switch between all kinds of channels, from FM radio to morse code. It’s truly an eavesdropper's paradise.


After connecting my SDR dongle and antenna to my laptop and CubicSDR, I was set up to pick up a satellite signal. The toolkit specifies the frequencies for the NOAA 15, NOAA 18, and NOAA 19 satellites. Then, I looked on this website to find predictions of exactly when each satellite would pass over my location, and which passes would be the most visible, and therefore the clearest signal to record. The satellite passes are around 12 minutes long and are successful if you manage to pick up a constant repeated beep.



The challenge of this process is to follow every step exactly - because the wrong angle, position, or length of the antenna could impact the success of your satellite pickup - which is what happened to me the first five times I tried to pick up a signal, and was met with a waterfall of static on my screen and through my speakers.




I got very frustrated when I couldn’t pick up the signal, and wondered what I’d done wrong.

I wrote this poem as a response to my frustration.

When starlings murmur
their bodies cascade in perfect formation
with the preciseness of an algorithm yet the
unpredictability of an avalanche.

When satellites murmur
their bodies cascade in perfect formation
with the preciseness of an algorithm yet the
unpredictability of an avalanche.

Mobile frequencies disrupt the dawn chorus
murmurations trip telephone wires
pet birds sing (Amazon’s)
‘Alexa’ wild birds sing chainsaws

I crave electromagnetic waves undulating through me
to hear the dawn chorus of cyborg swarms
energy transmission more potent than waxy ears can receive

What's an ear to an antenna?

Every night I make my pilgrimage to the sky
I stick out my (Amazon) antenna and frequencies cascade like an avalanche
I ascend into megahertz

and pray that you will sing back to me
with the cadence of birdsong
but all I get is a beeeeeep and some shshshshshshsh sounds
I will never learn your language.

I became desperate to receive a transmission. To find something, anything other than static.

After troubleshooting, and perhaps cosmic luck, I finally managed to pick up a satellite signal.

The NOAA 15 on April 4th, 2025, at 7.13-7.25PM. The beep was consistent and I pointed my antenna to the sky like an outstretched hand.

Sing back to me, to me, to me.

I used the open weather decoder , which converts the recorded WAV file into an Automatic Picture Transmission (APT), and I received this image below:


The static conceals the aerial view of the clouds and the earth, showing just a fragment of familiarity. In a city like NYC, and in most parts of the world, it is rare to avoid electromagnetic interference (you need to go to The National Radio Quiet Zone in West Virginia , a sanctuary of electromagnetic purity for astronomers and the military).


I feel that the visual translation of the static audio encapsulates the ephemeral aesthetic of this project. As mentioned earlier, if a murmuration can sonically resemble static, then maybe static is just as beautiful, just as integral to the narrative, as a signal?
