Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Deep Listening to the Nonhuman


“Can you imagine listening to all that is sounding as if your body were the whole earth? There might be the sounds of your own thoughts or of your body, natural sounds of birds or animals, voices, sounds of electrical appliances and machines. Some sounds might be very faint, some very intense, some continuous, and some intermittent. As you are listening globally, can you imagine that you can use any sound that you hear as a cue either to relax your body more deeply or to energize it?”

Pauline Oliveros


I start this section with a passage from Pauline Oliveros, a composer and pioneer of deep listening as a practice. Deep listening is a call to attention, an act of listening that brings heightened awareness to every part of a soundscape, where all sounds can be absorbed and embodied. In order to listen, or to attempt to listen to nonhuman voices, it is useful to follow this practice, to pick up the ephemeral beeps that come and go, to listen to the birds.

The cosmic soundscape is ephemeral and unpredictable, and has enthralled radio enthusiasts and UFO-hunters alike. In ‘The Secret Life of Electronic Objects’ , Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby reflect on the niche subculture of ‘whistler hunters’, who:


“…search out VLF (very low frequency) radio waves or ‘sferics’ short for ‘atmospherics’: natural radio-frequency emissions in the ionosphere, caused by electromagnetic energy radiated from lightning. These signals — resonant clicks and pops called ‘tweaks’ and ‘bonks’ by scientists — occur in the audible range and may be picked up by antennas and amplified for listening. Occasionally sferics get caught on, and travel long distances along the magnetic flux lines around the earth, producing ‘whistlers’, downward gliding signals which may last up to three seconds. These sounds are best received at night, far away from power lines and electromagnetic pollution. Whistler hunters travel far to unpolluted sites, sometimes camping out for days, listening for the elusive sounds of natural radio.

Although the sounds are fleetingly beautiful, out of context they lose much of their charm; their beauty is entwined with the effort endured and the symbolic significance of receiving them, which for some is quasi-mystical, for others a defiant gesture against people’s careless attitude towards nature.”


It seems to ‘whistler hunters’ and other radio amateurs, that the thrill in seeking out these sounds is as important as the signal received.


Another example of a mystical cosmic signal is the discovery earlier this year that ‘chorus waves’ - a type of ‘whistler’ - are in deep space, and sound remarkably like the dawn chorus of birdsong. That they have been found in deep space is significant because it suggests they can exist anywhere with the semblance of an electromagnetic field - and their ability to generate high-energy electrons makes them threatening to satellite communication as a source of interference. So, we have a whistler that sounds like a bird chirping that can interfere with satellite signals - what a network of mimicry!


Chorus waves were discovered back in World War I, when they were accidentally picked up by radio operators listening for enemy transmissions. A similar example is the now iconic ‘Songs of the Humpback Whale’ album by Roger Payne, which used recordings captured by a US navy engineer Frank Watlington, who picked up these haunting sounds while trying to detect Soviet submarines from a top-secret hydrophone station. There is a subversive beauty to the decontextualizing of such sounds from their military roots.


In 1977, the mysterious ‘WOW!’ signal was picked up by Ohio State University’s Big Ear radio telescope and lasted 72 seconds. The signal was strikingly intense and of a highly specific frequency, leading people to speculate that it was an extraterrestrial message. This has since been disputed by a more plausible counter theory - that the sound came from a hydrogen cloud unpredictably emitting a beam of light.



Regardless, the impact of the Wow! Signal reflects a wider cultural fascination with our search for extraterrestrial life. SETI has its origins in the pioneering work of astronomer Frank Drake who started searching for evidence of alien intelligence back in 1959, through radio transmission and reception. In 1974, the first interstellar radio transmission into space was sent from the Arecibo Observatory in Puerto Rico. The transmission was a simple pictogram of a human being and of a telescope, composed of binary code. Nadia Drake describes the effect of the transmission:


“Astronomers had already started eavesdropping on the heavens, hopefully awaiting murmurs from beyond that would break our seeming cosmic solitude. But this was something different - an intentional summons, perhaps an invitation for communion with hypothetical beings among the stars.”


Ted Chiang’s short story ‘The Great Silence’, approaches Arecibo’s search for extraterrestrial life from the perspective of a local parrot, who considers the search as hypocritical:


“The humans use Arecibo to look for extraterrestrial intelligence. Their desire to make a connection is so strong that they’ve created an ear capable of hearing across the universe. But I and my fellow parrots are right here. Why aren’t they interested in listening to our voices? We’re a nonhuman species capable of communicating with them. Aren’t we exactly what humans are looking for?”


Here lies the contradiction. This search for unknown and distant intelligence has always been idealized and mystified, while the natural world is taken for granted.


And yet, there are some humans who listen attentively to the animal voice - notably sound ecologist Bernie Krause. His album ‘The Great Animal Orchestra’, is a love letter to biophony, the collective sounds of nonhuman organisms sharing the same habitat. He has recorded thousands of species from Gonarezhou National Park in Zimbabwe to the Algonquin Provincial Park in Ontario, and over the years has documented the growing silence that has emerged in these natural soundscapes as a product of anthropogenic sound.


Silence is also explored by Rachel Carson in her 1962 book ‘Silent Spring’. She writes , “Over increasingly large areas of the United States, spring now comes unheralded by the return of birds, and the early mornings are strangely silent where once they were filled with the beauty of birdsong.” To her, silence is the first indicator of the loss of biodiversity, and she blames the impact of human destruction for this loss.


This passage from ‘Birdsong’ by Sigil explores the tension between biophonic and anthropogenic sound:


“What is the radio for?’ The Radio is for taming chicken. It is my method for raising chicken. When a chick is born, as soon as it is born, we bring a radio and play it at the maximum volume. In this manner, the bird becomes accustomed to loud sounds. Even if a car makes a sound, the bird will not become excitable. We often leave the radio on for seven days.”


It was written in response to the impact of Israeli occupation on not just humans - but birds - in Jawlan, a South Syrian territory. Here the anthropogenic sounds are more disruptive than range rovers and alarms - they are weapons of mass destruction and their impact is deafening. In Western Australia, the lyrebird, an excellent mimicker, has grown so accustomed so the melodies of deforestation, that it can imitate a chainsaw to an uncanny degree. Human interference disrupts the equilibrium of the natural soundscape that has clearly defined channels for animal vocalization.


In ‘Living as a Bird’, Viviance Despret writes about these channels - specifically for polyphonic birdsong. She writes about how birdsong relates to wider sonic geopolitics, as birds mark their territories based on sonic boundaries:

“As a result of this segmentation into sonic niches, of these partitioned voices, through which conflicts linked to acoustic territories are settled, the birds’ songs rarely overlap. Consequently, they become part of a new system, that of orchestration, not only in a strictly musical sense but also in the sense of a social musicality. Territories are orchestrations and melodic harmonies.”


The sound produced is neither cacophonic nor silent, but a coordinated chorus that enables birds to sing without signal jamming. Signal jamming is the act of a bird interrupting another bird’s song, often for the purpose of hijacking a mating call. We can compare signal jamming between birds to radio jamming, which is the deliberate interference of wireless signals to disrupt information transmission. It is achieved by attuning a transmitter to the same frequency of the opposing receiver, in order to override the signal. Radio jamming (different from interference because it is deliberate) is utilized as a form of censorship in totalitarian states and between warring countries. In China, it has been reported that shortwave radio transmissions of broadcasts such as the Voice of America and Radio Free Asia are jammed and replaced by Chinese folk music such as the Chinese Firedrake Jammer (you can listen to an example below)


I am fascinated by the politics surrounding signal jamming, both between birds and their electronic counterparts - transmitters. I want to relish the speculative possibility of birds signal jamming radio and satellite communication, even if just for a brief moment, to disrupt our human obsession with connectivity, speed, and growth, that is epitomized by Starlink.
