Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Process: Part Two - Theremin


A theremin is a perfect instrument to interact with celestial bodies - such as a satellite or potential ETI - because it operates on electromagnetic waves and antennas; it speaks the same language. A theremin has two antennas - one for pitch and one for volume - and is the only non-contact musical instrument in the world. The player uses their body to interfere with the electromagnetic field, which shifts the frequency or amplitude of the oscillators. It was invented in 1920 by Lev Sergeyevich Termen - better known as Leon Theremin - in a scientific laboratory in Soviet Russia.

Similar to the whale sounds picked up by US navy engineer Frank Watlington and transformed into a music album by Roger Payne, the theremin was a result of Soviet government-funded research into proximity sensors - the musical instrument emerged from military technology. Allegedly, Vladimir Lenin was so taken by it that Theremin was sent on an international tour to show off what is arguably the first electronic instrument and a model of successful Soviet technology. The story gets better - while Theremin was touring the US, he was engaging in industrial espionage for the Soviets, although years later in 1939 he returned to Russia and was arrested and sentenced for being counterrevolutionary. The tension between sound and music as a product of military application is a fascinating motif that I have discovered in my research, as explored in earlier anecdotes through Sputnik, the chorus waves, the humpback whale, and now, the theremin.


My favorite example of theremin usage is ‘Teen Age Message’. In 2001, led by scientist Alexander L. Zaitsev, a group of teens from the Moscow Center of Teen Activity transmitted a theremin concert into the cosmos from the Yevpatoria RT-70 radio telescope, with the hopes of communicating with extraterrestrial life through music and art. In his proposal to Arecibo Observatory, which was rejected due to a fear of making contact with nonhuman (or extraterrestrial) civilizations, he writes “I propose not a discrete two-tone radio signal but a more native continuous one, in which smooth frequency variations directly transfer one-dimensional emotional information. I am talking about music, which is more universally comprehensible than language, and about theremin, which is an electronic non-contact musical instrument.”





I have been intrigued and perplexed by the theremin since hearing it play unexpectedly at a festival, both for the haunting yet ethereal sound it produces, and also the detailed and delicate embodied movement it requires from a human player. I created a digital interactive theremin on TouchDesigner last year with oscillators and hand tracking, which was effective yet ultimately limited to the confines of my laptop screen and its hand tracking abilities.


After doing more research into the world of the theremin, I found the opportunity to attend a workshop run by world-renowned thereminist Dorit Chrysler, director and co-founder of the NYC Theremin Society.


I was able to play - for the first time - a physical theremin - specifically, a Moog Theremini. It was simultaneously underwhelming and overwhelming, for I was not a natural (can anyone be with a theremin when it requires you to play in an entirely novel way compared to any other instrument?) And yet, I was enchanted by its haunting presence, particularly when Dorit played and my whole body vibrated. After the workshop, I stayed to chat to her, and I shared my desire to incorporate the theremin as part of this cosmic network, to which she told me about ‘Electric Storm’, a performance she did at CERN - the European Organization for Nuclear Research - home to the biggest machine in the world: the Large Hadron Collider. Joe Silva describes ‘Electric Storm’ as a performance that:

“translates the feel of metal, chaos, laboratories, and the sound of thousands of voltages used to help cool the colliders, a tiny industrial revolution within the factory, capturing conceiving the moment of an idea, finally ending in one pure note of the Claravox (a type of theremin).”

This project is very inspiring in its exploration of the electronic interplay between theremin and industrial machine - the coldness of the machine contrasts with the sensitivity of the embodied instrument, and yet, the theremin can translate the sounds of the machine better than a human can.


After the workshop, I decided it was time to acquire a second-hand Moog Theremini, which I leaned towards more than a traditional theremin, because I would be able to use it as a midi controller connected to a digital interface.
