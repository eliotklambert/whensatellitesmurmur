Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Introduction

While the cosmos has captivated humanity with its elusive expansiveness for centuries, it is increasingly becoming a zone of interest for tech accelerationism. For centuries, the cosmos has captivated humanity with its elusive expansiveness. The cosmos is the passion project of both nature enthusiasts and tech accelerationists alike, although it is increasingly under threat of attempted colonization and extraction from the latter. I write this in the US in 2025, where satellite technology has been developed by SpaceX to create a network of global surveillance for the US government, under the guise of achieving global connectivity for its customers. It seems that the Faustian bargain of this information age is hyperconnectivity for the price of surveillance. To make matters worse, the new Trump administration is simultaneously undermining the use of satellite technology for scientific research; for instance, imposing cuts on the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) which uses satellites to predict weather data."

And yet, despite these dismal technopolitics, we are still captivated by by the dawn chorus, by mysterious satellite signals, by aerial murmurs of both the natural and artificial kind. The ephemerality of our cosmic soundscape is a lesson in deep listening, a chance to liberate technologies like radio and satellites from their military origins, and to repurpose them to celebrate the wonders of the nonhuman world. What sonic communication can be imagined between satellites, birds, and humans - one of harmony or interference? ‘When Satellites Murmur’ is an exploration of the tension between the poetics of cosmic ephemera and the politics of technology. The first section of the paper provides context for this exploration through the history of satellites, the relationship between satellites and their avian counterparts - starlings - and the world of deep listening and software defined radio. The second section of the paper documents my process of building a network between satellites, theremins, and birds for my final installation. This paper offers a momentary portal into the weird and wonderful world of the cosmos, and its artificial and natural nonhuman networks.
