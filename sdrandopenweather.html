Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Software Defined Radio

Much like satellites, radio is deeply embedded in the foundation of military communications, enabling operators to respond to a wide range of shifting protocols in real time. A particularly intriguing example of this is the history of number stations, which first emerged during World War I as a method of encrypted signaling via shortwave transmission. These cryptic broadcasts, often delivered by a chilling, monotone voice - frequently female - reciting seemingly random numbers, were then decrypted using techniques like the One-Time Pad, facilitating international communication between intelligence agencies.



While traditional radios tune into specific frequency bands, the birth of software-defined radio (SDR) allowed for more flexible modulation between frequencies and communication protocols, paving the way for richer experimentation. To its credit, SDR has made the often arcane world of radio more accessible and open-source to people who are interested in tuning in - which has encouraged a wave of radio amateurs to buy an antenna dongle kit, download software such as CubicSDR, and receive whichever frequency their heart so desires (in my case, between 500 kHz and 1.75 GHz as these are the specs of the RTL2832U kit).


My entry into SDR led me to the fascinating work of Open Weather , an intersectional feminist collective, informed by feminist theory and speculative storytelling, that uses SDR to collect weather forecasting data. On their website, they describe their ethos:

“In the tradition of intersectional feminism, open-weather investigates the politics of location and interlocking oppressions that shape our capacities to observe, negotiate, and respond to the climate crisis. In doing so, open-weather challenges dominant representations of earth and environment while complicating ideas of the weather beyond the meteorological.”



Their work feels particularly necessary during the Trump administration’s censorship and erasure of climate data. Federal agencies such as the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), which monitors the satellites that Open Weather track to produce their weather forecasting, have restricted or removed entirely access to climate-related datasets for scientists and the public. The future of global weather monitoring has been thrown into disarray - the administration has already substantially cut the funding and workforce of NOAA in favor of privatizing space exploration and scientific research, which will inevitably lead to poor and inaccurate science.


By building an archive of DIY weather data collection, similarly to Gregory Green’s ‘Gregnik’, more value is placed on community knowledge-building over state control. I decided to adopt Open Weather’s methodology to build my own DIY satellite ground station. My project really took a turn when I was introduced to their methodology, as picking up the satellite signals opened up a portal into the cosmic soundscape.
