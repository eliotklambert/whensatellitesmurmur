Eliot Lambert / When Satellites Murmur

Part Four - Experimentation

These stills were taken during my testing of the theremin/satellite dish/antenna setup. The first four were taken in my bedroom and roof, and the last two were taken in a belltower of a church on a floor that was lined with dead pigeons. This process was very experimental and I realized that I could play a theremin with an antenna! I also realized that playing the theremin as an instrument would be a lot more difficult than using it as a midi controller, because I was so new to learning it.


There was something meditative about this experimental phase, yet also frustrating because I had more technical issues and was facing a lot of wind, as well as meeting a lot of static when recording (although I got used to it). When I had less setup, or when my equipment wasn’t working (for example at the church where I only had my antenna unconnected to SDR), it was interesting to think more speculatively about what a signal could sound like. The act of picking up the NOAA signal is definitely more exciting than listening to it without context, so to imagine what invisible channels of sound the antennas could pick up, and how the signal could be modulated, were both integral parts of my sonic modulation.



Audio Reactive Visual Experiment in TouchDesigner


This audio-reactive TouchDesigner experiment channels audio from CubicSDR into TouchDesigner, where visual noise displacement causes the video of a starling murmuration to dissolve into static as the amplitude increases. I wanted to explore the idea that the murmuration doesn’t just sound like static, but can also be visually transformed into it—as thousands of birds become specks of noise on the screen. The murmuration is electronically processed.

The audio in this experiment comes from a website called Radio Reference, which lists the frequencies of the NYPD communication networks. Inspired by the work of Robin ‘Scanner’ Rimbaud, who uses shortwave radio and police scanners to record snippets of private conversations, I was drawn to eavesdropping on police channels, and was surprised that these channels were openly available to anyone with a shortwave radio.

This experiment was very evocative, and I wanted to incorporate it into my sonic network that decided to build on Max MSP instead of TouchDesigner, because the former allows for a much wider variety of audio parameters than the latter.
