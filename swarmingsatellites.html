Let us begin by delving into the history of satellites as instruments of warfare and surveillance. In 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1, the world’s first artificial satellite, equipped with four external antennas that transmitted rhythmic radio pulses back to Earth. The sound — short, repetitive beeps — could be picked up across the globe using simple shortwave receivers. To the U.S. government, this continuous signal represented more than a technical feat; it symbolized a Soviet presence that had transcended Earth’s boundaries and entered the realms of the cosmic. The resulting wave of alarm became known as the ‘Sputnik Crisis.’ That the signal was repetitive, omnipresent, accessible to anyone, and immune to jamming made it not just a scientific broadcast, but a political act.





In response, the United States launched Explorer 1 on January 31, 1958, followed by Vanguard 1 two months later, which was provocatively nicknamed the ‘grapefruit satellite’ by Soviet Premier Nikita Krushchev. Despite its unremarkable size, it holds the record as the longest-orbiting satellite in history!


40 years after Sputnik, Gregory Green responded with Gregnik , a model replica of Sputnik 1 that was mounted in Williamsburg, Brooklyn to broadcast artist-created messages over a low frequency radio signal. “It took the entire Soviet state to do Sputnik in 1957," says Green ."For some yahoo from Brooklyn to do the same thing 40 years later is to redefine our understanding of the individual." The dissemination of information is no longer in the hands of the state, but can be mediated by individual broadcasters like Green, and more widely recognized, revolutionary video artist Nam June Paik.


Paik’s ‘Good Morning Mr Orwell’ was a live satellite television broadcast between New York and Paris on New Year’s Day in 1984. The piece directly challenged the connotations of satellites as predicted by writer George Orwell’s ‘1984’ and actualized by the military and state as tools of surveillance. It united a staggering 25 million viewers worldwide in a collective watching and listening experience, to celebrate the wonders of performance art. My favorite part of the story is that the broadcast got progressively more chaotic as the night went on, due to delays, interference, the drunkenness of the NYC host George Plimpton, and the musician John Cage turning up high to his performance. Technically, the show was something of a disaster, but artistically and philosophically, it was a masterpiece.



However, despite these artists challenging the application of satellite technology, the reality in 2025 is looking bleak. Starlink, an internet constellation company owned by SpaceX, is on a mission to create ‘global connectivity’. The phrase sounds tantalizingly utopian, and yet, in this era of unprecedented technological oligarchy, it is hard to feel that ‘connectivity’ isn’t just a preferential synonym for ‘surveillance’. It seems that the faustian bargain of this information age is surveillance for the price of connectivity. I imagine the cosmos as a royal court and satellites as sycophants, circling around, secretly spying on and betraying others. In a contemporary context, Starling satellites act as disciples of Elon Musk and SpaceX. In 2021, the National Reconnaissance Office (NRO), a US government agency which, according to its website, "makes the world a better, safer, stronger place and ensures America remains the undisputed leader in space,” contracted SpaceX to build a swarm network of hundreds of spy satellites to advance global surveillance. On March 16 2024, Reuters disclosed:


“...for the first time that the SpaceX contract is for a powerful new spy system with hundreds of satellites bearing Earth-imaging capabilities that can operate as a swarm in low orbits, and that the spy agency that Musk's company is working with is the NRO.”


What is a satellite swarm? It is a formation of synchronized spacecraft that follows the rules of natural flocking behavior. Flocking behavior has impressed scientists and mathematicians for decades, one of the most notable examples being the ‘Boid Algorithm,’ developed by computer scientist Craig W. Reynolds in 1987, who writes in his seminal paper - ‘Flocks, Herds, and Schools: A Distributed Behavioral Model’ - “I would like to thank flocks. herds. and schools for existing; nature is the ultimate source of inspiration for computer graphics and animation.” This theoretical model, often used to create simulations, follows three rules of movement - separation, to avoid collisions, alignment, to match velocity, and cohesion, to avoid predators. Although the model is only theoretical, it can be applied to real-life satellite swarm configurations, for example Starlink spacecraft, which are programed to synchronize data collection, adapt to fast-changing protocols, and avoid collisions.



In 2023, NASA launched Project Starling , a ten-month mission of four satellite spacecraft orbiting as a swarm, with the intention that they would communicate autonomously without relying on a delayed ground signal. Much like Apple and Amazon, the name 'Project Starling' implies that communication technology is a natural and organic phenomenon. A tactical move, considering starling murmurations are one of the most naturally spectacular phenomena on planet earth. Satellite swarms, by contrast, have been meticulously programmed.


A murmuration is a swarm of thousands of starlings whose dynamic, shape-shifting performance is more than just flocking. They paradoxically move unpredictably yet algorithmically. The term 'murmuration' derives from the distinctive murmuring sound produced by the simultaneous flapping of thousands of wings. The collective sound of a swarm - as explored by Eugene Thacker in 'Pulse Demons' - is as important as its visual symbolism, if not more so. He writes:


“...the problem is that swarms are not only topologically complex, but phenomenally complex as well. For example, cicadas constitute a swarm, not because of their coordinated, bodily movements, but because of the collective sound they produce. In fact, cicadas are more often heard than seen – indeed, they are quite impossible to locate by sound. Even in cases where swarming is visually present – locusts or large-scale starling flocks – the swarm is a massive acoustic event as well. There is something ‘living’ here, but something that may have nothing to do with biology or the organic..."


Thacker's insight reveals a deeper connection between sound and swarm behavior. The sound of the murmuration - of the flapping of thousands of wings - evokes for me something akin to radio static, which is the sonic product of electromagnetic interference. In my mind, this brings the murmuration closer to the umwelt of the antenna, which operates on electromagnetism. The swarm’s movements mirror an algorithmic precision, where each bird's motion seems to be calculated to the smallest detail, giving the impression of a digital simulation. Thacker’s assertion that the swarm is ‘living’ yet separate from the biological or organic realm aligns with this interpretation.


And what better example to demonstrate this tension between the organic and inorganic than a bird spy drone?


In 2024, the People’s Liberation Army of China unveiled a spy drone disguised as a sparrow, taking biomimicry to an unprecedented level. It is uncannily birdlike, intended to blend in with its real counterparts. The prophecy of ‘Birds Aren’t Real’ , the satirical conspiracy theory that swept the US in 2017, claiming that the government had replaced real birds with drones, is unironically coming true. My first thought upon discovering this, besides my fear that surveillance capitalism is growing exponentially, was how real birds would respond to this fraudster. Would they fear it? Attack it? Sing to it? Would they immediately recognize its otherness?



The natural world is not entirely resigned to the whims of the information age, described by The Xenofeminist Manifesto as “...the monstrous complexity of our reality, a reality crosshatched with fiber-optic cables, radio and microwaves, oil and gas pipelines, aerial and shipping routes, and the unrelenting, simultaneous execution of millions of communication protocols with every passing millisecond.” Whether intentional or unintentional, cases of animals clashing with technological systems - such as orcas sinking yachts in southwest Europe, starlings tripping telephone wires, and sharks chewing internet cables - provide respite to the relentlessness of accelerationism. The effect may only be momentary - before returning to business as usual - but it serves as a cautionary tale. If humans carry on taking the natural world for granted, the nonhuman world will slowly but surely fight back - probably not to the extent of Alfred Hitchcock’s eye-pecking birds in ‘The Birds’ , but in ways that are inevitable as humans carry on disrupting and extracting.


To respond to the ‘monstrous complexity of our reality’, we need to slow down and listen to nonhumans that have outlived and will continue to outlive us.
